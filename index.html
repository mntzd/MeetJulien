Why do I want to learn coding?

Get to know more about me & my motivation.

During my studies of psychology I was contemplating how could I combine my interest and academic background revolving around the human psyche with my passion and fascination for technology? I tried to learn basic technical concepts by myself, but programming is hard and my studies kept my busy, so I was not able to achieve a level that would allow me to create value for other through technology.

In the early 2010's, I was lucky enough to be an intern for a professor of "Human Computer Interaction" (HCI) and "Mobile Information Systems". The internship was tough and I fucked up on multiple occasions, but it helped me grow my interest and knowledge into the [for psychologists] niche direction of Usability and User Experience Design.

Digging deeper and deeper into the field of HCI & UX, I began to plan my thesis around a data-driven behaviour analysis of car-drivers and ended up partnering with TomTom to optimize specific issues in their product. The navigation system's algorithm produced an usability issue in certain situations, so I analyzed human problem solving strategies in similar situations and re-applied them on the system & algorithm. Up to this point all my work was conceptual, since I still did not know how to properly apply programming and technical analysis to solve complex problems. I felt like there was a blind-spot to my results, and I was reliant on other people to make sure the conceptualizations were also fit for realization.

7 years ago I started working as a junior UX Designer in one of Berlins Product Agencies. The demand was there, not only for UX but also for Product Owners (POs), so the next logical thing after working for a few months as UX-Designer was to additionally get certified as PO. As part of a Development Team we developed multiple cool things, and I was constantly amazed by the things our code-magicians could do to create some ~real~ value for customers & users.

During the first 2 years in the agency my learning curve skyrocketed (and I loved it) but unfortunately we grew too big too fast and our biggest client pulled out. So the agency closed its doors for good. This was when a former colleague, who was building a design & prodcut consulting team for a large international technology consultancy, offered me to check out the consulting world.

The shift from product to business, agency to consulting, was tougher than expected, but I eventually adapted. More Workshops and even more concept-work, a large variety of clients and the opportunity to closely interact with our own startup-hub made this a very interesting experience with lots of personal growth and prosperous personal connections. After 2 years though, time was ripe for something new. One of the Big-4 consultancies planned to build a design capability (and Team) in Germany, so I joined as their Lead-UX-Designer. With the projects and programs in the consultancy I was exposed to large tech-projects and could apply my basic techn-skills to build prototypes (along the other conceptual and UX work), but I never even touched the quality then for production level code or features. I wanted to further grow into this direction, but being very busy with projects, managing a team and the overall culture made that close to impossible.
While we may have had success from a business perspective, the rate of new learnings decreased by the day, since the only thing you will get measured in is money earned. Especially in the large software projects I felt like "just" being a designer is not sufficient. I wanted to close the gap, to build the bridge between concept and creation. But my job did not allow for that, so I pulled the brakes. I am grateful for the opportunities to grow and will re-focus on what is important to me: exploring new things in life, finding joy through learning and evolving.

My personal goal is to evolve towards being a "Full Stack Product Person" - meaning I can not only make informed decisions in the different dimensions of the product creation process, but be an active and defining part.
